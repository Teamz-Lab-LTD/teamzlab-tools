How Vintage Photo Effects Work

Each era preset applies a sequence of pixel-level transformations using the Canvas API in your browser. The 1920s preset converts to luminance-weighted greyscale (the same formula used by orthochromatic film: 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B), then adds high-frequency luminance noise that mimics the grain pattern of nitrate film stock. The 1950s preset applies a warm sepia tone by mapping greyscale to a warm brown-cream gradient, with a gentle contrast lift matching the paper tone of mid-century prints. Last updated: March 2026.

The 1970s preset lifts the black point (faded-look), applies a warm orange-yellow colour cast, and reduces saturation slightly — exactly the look of expired Kodachrome or Ektachrome film. The 1990s preset applies boosted saturation and contrast with subtle grain, mimicking consumer film of that era like Fujifilm Superia or Kodak Gold.

Era Presets Explained

  • 1920s — Black & White + Grain: Orthochromatic greyscale conversion with luminance noise. Period-accurate contrast ratio matching glass-plate and early silver gelatin prints.
  • 1950s — Warm Sepia: Warm tonal mapping from greyscale to a brown-cream gradient. Matches the look of albumen or gelatin silver prints with paper yellowing.
  • 1970s — Faded Film: Lifted blacks, orange-warm cast, reduced saturation. Matches expired Kodachrome/Ektachrome slide film and early colour negative stock.
  • 1990s — Nostalgic Film: Boosted saturation, lifted midtones, light grain. Matches consumer print film from the mass-market photography era.

Vintage Photo vs Modern Photo Comparison

Modern digital photos have flat, clean tonality. Vintage film photography had distinct colour science per emulsion type, paper surface, and age. These presets capture the most recognisable visual signatures of each era — not just a colour filter, but the specific contrast curve, grain structure, and colour cast that makes each era's photography immediately recognisable to viewers.
