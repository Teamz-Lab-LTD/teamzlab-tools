How Pay Raise Calculation Works

A pay raise calculator converts a percentage increase into real dollar amounts across every pay period. Enter your current annual salary and the raise percentage to instantly see your new annual salary, the raise amount, and how it breaks down into monthly, biweekly, weekly, and hourly figures. The calculator also estimates the after-tax value of your raise based on your marginal federal tax bracket, giving you a realistic picture of how much extra money actually reaches your bank account each month.

The core formula is straightforward: new salary equals current salary multiplied by one plus the raise percentage divided by 100. From there, dividing by 12 gives the monthly figure, dividing by 26 gives the biweekly paycheck amount, dividing by 52 gives the weekly rate, and dividing by total annual work hours gives the hourly rate. The after-tax estimate multiplies the raise amount by one minus your marginal tax rate, which approximates the federal tax impact on the incremental income from your raise.

Understanding Your Raise After Taxes

A common mistake during salary negotiations is focusing only on the gross raise percentage without considering the tax impact. In the United States, federal income tax brackets for 2026 range from 10% to 37%, and your raise is taxed at your marginal rate because it represents additional income on top of your existing salary. For example, if you earn $80,000 and receive a 10% raise of $8,000, and your marginal bracket is 22%, the after-tax value of that raise is approximately $6,240 per year or $520 per month. This calculator shows both gross and after-tax figures so you can set realistic expectations for your increased take-home pay.

Keep in mind that this estimate covers federal tax only. State income taxes, Social Security contributions (6.2% up to the wage base), and Medicare taxes (1.45%) further reduce take-home pay. However, even as an approximation, the marginal tax bracket provides a useful baseline for comparing raise offers and understanding the real-world impact of a salary increase.

Inflation and Real Purchasing Power

Not every raise translates into greater purchasing power. If inflation runs at 3% per year and your raise is only 2%, your real income has actually decreased. This calculator includes an inflation-adjusted comparison that shows whether your raise outpaces a 3% inflation benchmark. A raise that merely matches inflation is a cost-of-living adjustment that keeps you even, while a raise above inflation represents a genuine improvement in your standard of living. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, average annual inflation in the US has ranged from 2% to 8% over the past decade, making this comparison essential for evaluating any raise offer.

When to Use This Calculator

Use the pay raise calculator during annual performance reviews to understand what a proposed percentage means in dollar terms. It is equally useful when comparing job offers, negotiating a promotion, or evaluating a cost-of-living adjustment. Hiring managers and HR professionals can use it to model compensation scenarios and present raise proposals in concrete terms. The hourly rate breakdown is especially valuable for salaried employees who want to compare their effective rate against freelance or contract opportunities, ensuring they understand their total compensation on an apples-to-apples basis.
