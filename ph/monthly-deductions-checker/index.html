Complete Philippine Payroll Deductions Explained

Philippine payroll deductions consist of two main categories: mandatory government contributions (SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG) and withholding tax. Understanding both categories is essential for employees who want to know exactly how their gross salary translates into net take-home pay. While government contributions are fixed based on rates set by the respective agencies, withholding tax varies significantly based on the employee's taxable income, filing status, and number of qualified dependents. Together, these deductions can represent a substantial portion of an employee's gross pay, making it critical to calculate them accurately.

The Philippine income tax system under the TRAIN Law (Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion, Republic Act No. 10963) uses a graduated tax rate structure with brackets ranging from 0 percent to 35 percent. Employees earning up to 250,000 pesos annually (approximately 20,833 pesos monthly) are exempt from income tax. Above this threshold, tax rates progressively increase: 15 percent on income between 250,001 and 400,000 pesos, 20 percent on income between 400,001 and 800,000 pesos, 25 percent on income between 800,001 and 2,000,000 pesos, 30 percent on income between 2,000,001 and 8,000,000 pesos, and 32 percent on income between 8,000,001 and the upper bracket, with 35 percent applying to income above that. These rates apply to the taxable income after deducting mandatory contributions.

The computation of withholding tax starts with the gross monthly salary, from which the employee's share of SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG are deducted to arrive at the taxable income. This means that government contributions effectively reduce your tax base, providing a small tax benefit. The taxable income is then annualised (multiplied by 12) to determine the applicable tax bracket, and the resulting annual tax is divided by 12 to get the monthly withholding tax. Employers are responsible for computing and remitting this withholding tax to the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) on behalf of their employees.

Filing status affects the tax computation in the Philippines. Single filers and married filers are subject to the same graduated tax rates under the TRAIN Law, which simplified the system by eliminating the separate tax tables for different filing statuses that existed before. However, qualified dependents can still affect the computation through certain exemptions and deductions available to taxpayers with dependents, though the personal and additional exemptions were replaced by the standard 250,000-peso annual tax exemption under TRAIN. Despite this simplification, understanding your filing status remains important for accurate payroll processing and annual tax return filing.

How to Verify Your Payroll Deductions

Employees should regularly review their pay slips to ensure that deductions are calculated correctly. Compare the figures on your pay slip with the results from this calculator. If you notice discrepancies, raise them with your employer's payroll department. Common sources of error include outdated contribution tables, incorrect salary credit brackets for SSS, and failure to update PhilHealth premium rates. Your employer is legally obligated to provide you with a detailed pay slip showing all deductions, and you have the right to question any amount that appears incorrect. For annual verification, compare your BIR Form 2316 (Certificate of Compensation Payment/Tax Withheld) with your own calculations to ensure everything aligns.
