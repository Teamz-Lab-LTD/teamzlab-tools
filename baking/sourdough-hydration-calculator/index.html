How Sourdough Hydration Works

Sourdough hydration is expressed as a percentage calculated using baker's math, where flour is always 100% and everything else is measured relative to it. Hydration percentage equals total water divided by total flour, multiplied by 100. The key insight most bakers miss is that sourdough starter contributes both flour and water to the final dough. A 100% hydration starter is half flour and half water by weight, so 100 grams of starter adds 50 grams of flour and 50 grams of water to your recipe. Ignoring these contributions can throw off your true hydration by several percentage points, leading to unexpected dough texture.

Understanding true hydration gives you precise control over your bread's crumb structure, crust development, and handling characteristics. Lower hydration doughs around 60-65% are stiff and easy to shape, producing a tight crumb ideal for bagels and pretzels. Higher hydration doughs above 75% are slack and sticky but reward you with an open, airy crumb structure that characterizes artisan breads like ciabatta and focaccia. This calculator accounts for every gram of flour and water in your recipe, including what your starter contributes.

Hydration Guide for Different Breads

Each bread style has a target hydration range developed through centuries of baking tradition. Bagels and pretzels thrive at 60-65% hydration, where the stiff dough holds its shape during boiling and baking. Sandwich loaves perform best at 65-70%, giving a soft but structured crumb that slices cleanly. Country sourdough, the most popular artisan style, targets 70-75% for a balance of open crumb and manageable handling. Ciabatta pushes to 75-80%, and focaccia goes even higher at 80-85%, producing the characteristic large, irregular holes that trap olive oil and herbs.

When using this calculator's presets, the values are starting points. Your specific flour absorbs water differently depending on protein content, age, and type. Whole wheat and rye flours absorb more water than white bread flour, so you may need to increase hydration by 3-5% when substituting. Conversely, lower-protein flours like all-purpose may need slightly less water. Keep notes on what works with your specific flour and adjust the presets accordingly for consistently great results.

Using the Reverse Calculator

The reverse calculator solves the most common question in sourdough baking: how much water do I need for a specific hydration target? Enter your desired total flour weight, target hydration percentage, and starter details, and the calculator works backward to give you the exact recipe water amount. This is invaluable when adapting recipes between different starter hydrations or scaling a recipe to a new size while maintaining the same dough characteristics.

For example, if you want a 75% hydration country loaf with 500 grams total flour and 20% starter at 100% hydration, the reverse calculator determines that your starter contributes 50 grams of flour and 50 grams of water, so you need 450 grams of recipe flour and 325 grams of recipe water. This precision eliminates the guesswork that leads to dough that is too wet or too dry, especially when experimenting with new recipes or adjusting for different flour types and environmental conditions.

Tips for Consistent Sourdough Results

Always weigh your ingredients with a digital kitchen scale accurate to one gram. Volume measurements are unreliable for bread baking because flour density varies with how it is scooped and settled. Keep your starter at a consistent hydration so the calculator's math stays accurate. Most bakers maintain a 100% hydration starter because the equal flour-to-water ratio simplifies calculations, but any hydration works as long as you enter it correctly. Feed your starter 4-12 hours before mixing for peak activity, and note that a bubbly, doubled starter ferments dough faster than one just past its peak.

Temperature affects dough hydration behavior significantly. Warmer environments make dough feel wetter and more extensible, while cooler conditions make the same hydration feel drier and stiffer. If your kitchen runs hot in summer, consider reducing water by 5-10 grams from what the calculator recommends. In winter, you might add a few extra grams. Autolyse your flour and water for 30-60 minutes before adding starter and salt to improve gluten development and water absorption, which makes high-hydration doughs much easier to handle during shaping.
