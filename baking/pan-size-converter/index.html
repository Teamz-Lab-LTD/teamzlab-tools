How the Pan Size Converter Works

This pan size converter calculates the volume of your original pan and your target pan using standard geometric formulas. Round pans use the cylinder formula (pi times radius squared times height), while square and rectangular pans use length times width times height. The ratio between the two volumes gives you a precise scaling factor. Multiply every ingredient in your recipe by this factor and you will have the correct amounts for the new pan. The converter also adjusts baking time and temperature because pan size directly affects how heat reaches the center of your batter.

Switching between pan shapes is one of the most common baking challenges. A recipe written for a 9-inch round cake pan might need to work in an 8-inch square pan or a 9-by-13-inch rectangle. Without the correct volume adjustment, you risk overfilling a smaller pan (causing overflow) or underfilling a larger one (producing a thin, dry result). This tool eliminates the guesswork by giving you exact scaling factors and practical tips for every conversion.

Pan Size Conversions for Common Baking Swaps

The most frequently used pan conversion is 9-inch round to 8-inch square. These two pans have nearly identical volumes (about 8 cups each), making them almost perfectly interchangeable without any recipe adjustment. A 9-by-13-inch rectangular pan holds roughly the same volume as two 9-inch round pans, which is why layer cake recipes often translate directly to sheet cake form.

Bundt pans hold significantly more batter than standard round pans. A 10-inch Bundt pan holds about 12 cups, equivalent to roughly 1.5 times a 9-inch round. When scaling up to a Bundt, you typically need to increase the recipe by 50 percent and extend baking time by 15 to 25 minutes because the thick ring shape takes longer to bake through. Loaf pans present a similar challenge because their tall, narrow shape means longer baking at a slightly lower temperature for even doneness.

Adjusting Baking Time and Temperature

When you scale to a larger pan, the batter layer becomes thinner, which means it bakes faster. As a general rule, reduce baking time by about 10 to 15 percent when moving to a significantly larger pan. Conversely, a smaller pan produces a thicker batter layer that needs more time in the oven, so add 10 to 15 percent to the original time. Always start checking for doneness about 5 minutes before the adjusted time and use the toothpick test rather than relying solely on time.

Temperature adjustments are less common but important for extreme size changes. If you are scaling down to a much smaller pan (like mini loaf pans), consider lowering the oven temperature by 25 degrees Fahrenheit to prevent the outside from overbrowning before the center is done. For very large or deep pans like tube pans, the standard temperature usually works fine, but covering with foil partway through baking can prevent overbrowning on top while the center catches up.

Tips for Successful Pan Substitutions

Always fill pans only two-thirds full to allow room for rising. If your scaled recipe produces more batter than the new pan can safely hold, bake the extra in a small ramekin or muffin tin alongside the main pan. Grease and flour the pan thoroughly when switching shapes, especially with Bundt pans where intricate designs can trap batter. Glass and dark metal pans absorb more heat than light-colored aluminum, so reduce the oven temperature by 25 degrees when using these materials. Let your baked goods cool in the pan for the recommended time before turning out to prevent sticking or breaking, which is especially important when using an unfamiliar pan shape.
