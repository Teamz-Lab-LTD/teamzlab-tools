What Counts as a Rush Project?

A rush project is any work that requires you to deviate from your normal scheduling rhythm: compressing a multi-week project into days, working evenings or weekends, or bumping other paying clients to fit the urgent work. Industry-standard rush premiums start at 25% for tight deadlines (under 7 days) and climb to 100% or more for true emergencies (under 24 hours or weekend/overnight). These premiums are not greed — they are compensation for pipeline disruption, lost opportunity, reduced quality time on existing projects, and the personal cost of overtime hours.

This calculator blends three inputs into a defensible premium: urgency level (sets the baseline), workload impact (how much other billable work you would have to bump), and skill scarcity (how rare your skill set is for this type of work). The output is a specific recommended premium percentage, the final rush fee, and a go/no-go decision framework comparing total compensation to your opportunity cost.

Industry-Standard Rush Premium Tiers

Baseline Premiums by Urgency:
Standard timeline — 0% (no premium)
Tight deadline (< 7 days) — +25%
Rush (< 72 hours) — +50%
Emergency (< 24 hours) — +100%
Weekend / overnight — +100%

Adjustments:
Workload impact: +0.3% per 1% workload displaced
Skill scarcity: +0.2% per 1% scarcity

Final Fee:
Final Fee = Base Fee × (1 + Total Premium %)

These tiers come from widely-published freelance rate surveys (AIGA, Writers Guild, Graphic Artists Guild Handbook). Agencies and studios use similar or higher premiums internally, with emergency weekend work at 150-200% of standard rates. As an independent freelancer charging these premiums puts you in line with professional studio norms.

The Go / No-Go Decision Framework

Even a correctly-priced rush project is not always worth taking. Run this quick check: will the rush fee cover your opportunity cost (the revenue you lose from bumped clients plus the personal cost of compressed work)? If the final fee is less than 1.5x your normal project value, lean toward declining — the strain on your pipeline and reputation with existing clients is often not worth the modest upside. If the fee is 2x or more, accept with a signed rush addendum. For premiums between these ranges, evaluate strategically: is this a dream client, a portfolio-worthy project, or leverage for future work? Those qualitative factors can tip a marginal project into the accept column.

Always get rush acceptance in writing before starting work. Include the rush premium on the invoice as a separate line item so the client understands what they paid for and why. Clients who rush you once often rush you again — if you accept without surcharge the first time, you train them to expect free urgency forever.

When to Use This Calculator

Use this calculator every time a client asks for an "ASAP", "yesterday", or "end of week" turnaround on work that would normally take longer. Run the numbers before quoting so you arrive at a defensible figure grounded in industry norms rather than an on-the-spot guess. Share the breakdown with the client if they push back — a transparent premium broken down by urgency, workload disruption, and skill scarcity is much harder to argue with than a round markup number.
