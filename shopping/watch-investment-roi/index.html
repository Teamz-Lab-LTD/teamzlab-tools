Watches as an Investment

Luxury watches have increasingly been viewed as alternative investments alongside traditional assets like stocks and bonds. Certain timepieces, particularly limited-edition models from prestigious Swiss manufacturers, have shown remarkable appreciation over the past decade. A Rolex Daytona purchased at retail in 2019 for $13,000 could be worth over $30,000 today, delivering returns that rival or exceed the stock market. However, watch investment is not without nuance. Unlike dividend-paying stocks, watches require ongoing maintenance — including service costs every 5-7 years, insurance, and safe storage. This calculator helps you see the complete picture by factoring in these costs and comparing your net return against major asset classes.

Which Watch Brands Appreciate the Most?

Not all watches are created equal when it comes to investment potential. Historically, three brands dominate the appreciation charts: Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet. Rolex's sports models — the Daytona, Submariner, and GMT-Master II — have consistently appreciated due to limited supply and massive global demand. Patek Philippe's Nautilus and Aquanaut lines have seen extraordinary growth, with some references doubling in value within a few years. Audemars Piguet's Royal Oak remains highly sought after. Beyond the top three, independent brands like F.P. Journe, A. Lange & Sohne, and certain vintage Omega references have also shown strong returns. The key factors driving appreciation are: limited production numbers, iconic design, brand heritage, condition, and completeness of the original box and papers.

Risks of Watch Investing

While some watches have delivered extraordinary returns, it is important to understand the risks. The secondary market is cyclical — prices surged during 2020-2022 but corrected significantly in 2023-2024. Liquidity is limited compared to stocks; selling a watch can take weeks or months and involve dealer margins of 10-20%. Counterfeits are a constant concern, and condition issues like scratches or water damage can dramatically reduce value. Maintenance costs are real: a full Rolex service runs $800-$1,500, and Patek Philippe services can exceed $2,000. Storage and insurance add further costs. Most financial advisors recommend treating watches as a passion investment — buy what you love to wear, and view any appreciation as a bonus rather than a guaranteed return.
