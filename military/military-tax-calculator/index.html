How Military Tax Calculator Works

Military compensation includes several components, and not all of them are subject to federal income tax. Base pay and special pays are taxable, but two major allowances — Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) and Basic Allowance for Subsistence (BAS) — are completely tax-free. This tax-free status is one of the most significant financial benefits of military service, effectively increasing your take-home pay compared to a civilian earning the same gross salary.

Service members deployed to designated combat zones receive an even greater tax benefit: all military compensation, including base pay and special pays, becomes tax-free for the duration of the combat zone deployment. This combat zone tax exclusion (CZTE) can result in thousands of dollars in tax savings, making deployment a significant financial opportunity for service members who manage their money wisely.

Tax-Free Military Allowances

BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing) provides tax-free compensation based on your duty station location, pay grade, and dependent status. BAS (Basic Allowance for Subsistence) provides tax-free compensation for meals. Together, these allowances can represent 25-40% of total military compensation, and since they are entirely tax-free, they provide substantial tax savings compared to equivalent civilian earnings.

For example, an E-6 with dependents might receive $2,200/month in BAH and $452/month in BAS. That is $31,824 per year in tax-free income. A civilian would need to earn approximately $40,000-$45,000 in gross salary to have the same after-tax value, depending on their tax bracket.

Combat Zone Tax Exclusion

When deployed to a designated combat zone, all military pay becomes tax-free for enlisted members. For officers, the exclusion is capped at the highest enlisted pay plus hostile fire/imminent danger pay. The combat zone tax exclusion applies for any month in which the service member spent at least one day in the combat zone. This means even a single day in a combat zone at the end of a month makes the entire month's pay tax-free.

State Tax Considerations

Several states have no income tax on military pay, including Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska, Tennessee, and New Hampshire. Under the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA), military members are taxed by their state of legal residence, not their state of duty station. This means choosing a tax-free state as your home of record can eliminate state income tax entirely. Many service members establish legal residence in tax-free states to maximize their take-home pay throughout their career.
