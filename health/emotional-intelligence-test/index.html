What Is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional intelligence, often abbreviated as EQ or EI, is the capacity to be aware of, control, and express one's emotions and to handle interpersonal relationships judiciously and empathetically. The concept was popularized by psychologist Daniel Goleman in his 1995 bestseller, though the term was coined by researchers Peter Salovey and John Mayer in 1990. Unlike IQ, which is relatively stable throughout life, emotional intelligence is a set of skills that can be learned, practiced, and improved at any age.

The 5 Domains of Emotional Intelligence

Self-Awareness is the foundation of all emotional intelligence. It involves recognizing your own emotions as they happen, understanding how they affect your thoughts and behavior, and knowing your strengths and weaknesses. People with high self-awareness make better decisions because they understand their emotional triggers and biases.

Self-Regulation: Managing Your Emotions

Self-regulation is the ability to manage disruptive emotions and impulses. It does not mean suppressing emotions but rather choosing how and when to express them. People with strong self-regulation think before acting, remain calm under pressure, and adapt to changing circumstances without emotional meltdowns. This skill is critical in leadership, conflict resolution, and maintaining long-term relationships.

Motivation: The Inner Drive

In the context of EQ, motivation refers to intrinsic motivation — being driven by internal values, curiosity, and a desire to achieve rather than external rewards like money or status. Highly motivated people set challenging goals, persist through setbacks, and maintain optimism even when facing difficulties. This domain separates people who merely go through the motions from those who are truly engaged in their work and life.

Empathy: Understanding Others

Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of another person. It goes beyond sympathy, which is feeling sorry for someone, to actually perceiving and resonating with their emotional experience. Empathetic people are skilled at reading social cues, understanding unspoken feelings, and responding appropriately to others' emotional states. In the workplace, empathy improves teamwork, customer relations, and conflict management.

Social Skills: Managing Relationships

Social skills in the EQ framework encompass the ability to manage relationships, build networks, find common ground, and inspire others. This includes clear communication, effective conflict management, collaboration, teamwork, and the ability to influence and lead. People with strong social skills are often described as natural leaders, even if they do not hold formal leadership positions.

Why EQ Matters More Than You Think

Research consistently shows that EQ is a stronger predictor of workplace success, leadership effectiveness, and relationship satisfaction than IQ. A study by TalentSmart found that EQ accounts for 58 percent of job performance across all types of roles. Leaders with high EQ create more engaged teams, experience lower turnover, and achieve better business outcomes. In personal relationships, EQ determines how well partners communicate, resolve conflicts, and maintain emotional intimacy over time. The good news is that unlike IQ, emotional intelligence can be deliberately improved at any age through consistent practice, self-reflection, and feedback from others.
