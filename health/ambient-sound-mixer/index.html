White Noise vs Pink Noise vs Brown Noise

White noise contains equal energy at every frequency, producing a bright, hissing sound similar to television static. It is effective at masking high-pitched disturbances but can feel harsh over long listening sessions. Pink noise follows a 1/f power spectrum — each octave carries equal energy, giving it a warmer, deeper quality that resembles steady rainfall. Many sleep researchers consider pink noise the most natural-sounding option. Brown noise (also called Brownian or red noise) follows a 1/f-squared spectrum with even more bass emphasis, creating a deep rumble like distant thunder or a powerful waterfall. If you find white noise too sharp for sleeping, try switching to pink or brown noise — most people discover that deeper frequencies are more conducive to rest.
