How DDR5 and DDR4 Latency Differs in Real Nanoseconds

Raw MHz or MT/s speed is not the full story when comparing RAM. The CAS latency (CL) number determines how many clock cycles the memory controller must wait before data is delivered. Because faster RAM runs at shorter clock cycles, a high-CL kit at high frequency can end up slower than a low-CL kit at moderate speed.

The formula that reveals true latency: True Latency (ns) = (CAS Latency × 2000) ÷ Speed MT/s. A classic example that surprises builders: DDR4-3200 CL16 = (16 × 2000) ÷ 3200 = 10.0 ns, and DDR5-6000 CL30 = (30 × 2000) ÷ 6000 = 10.0 ns — identical real latency, despite the DDR5 kit running at nearly double the speed. However, DDR5-6000 CL28 = 9.3 ns — actually faster. This is why raw speed numbers mislead buyers who ignore CAS latency. Last updated: March 2026.

DDR5 vs DDR4 Gaming Performance — What the Benchmarks Show

For gaming at 1440p and 4K, RAM latency has minimal impact on frame rates — benchmarks from Tom's Hardware and Hardware Unboxed consistently show a 1–3% FPS difference between the best and worst mainstream RAM configurations. The GPU becomes the bottleneck well before RAM makes a measurable difference.

At 1080p with a top-tier CPU like the AMD Ryzen 7 7800X3D, RAM latency matters more. The 3D V-Cache architecture on this processor is particularly sensitive to memory latency, and the gap between best and worst RAM can reach 5–8% in CPU-bound esports titles. For content creation — video rendering and 3D modeling — DDR5's bandwidth advantage becomes significant, with DDR5 platforms delivering up to 40–60% more memory bandwidth than DDR4 at comparable price points. Programming and development workloads show minimal difference between DDR4 and DDR5.

When Is Upgrading to DDR5 Worth It in 2026?

DDR5 is the clear choice if you are building a new system on Intel Arrow Lake or AMD Ryzen 9000 — both platforms require DDR5. For these builds, DDR5-6000 CL28 or CL30 kits represent the best price-to-performance balance. If you do heavy content creation alongside gaming, DDR5's bandwidth advantage justifies the switch even on platforms that support both types.

DDR4 remains fully competitive if you already own a DDR4-3600 CL16 or better kit — the real latency advantage over DDR5 entry-level kits makes an upgrade pointless without a full platform change. For pure gaming at 1440p or 4K on an existing AM4 or LGA1700 system, upgrading RAM type requires a new motherboard and CPU, rarely making it cost-effective. Price context as of March 2026: DDR5 prices dropped 7.2% from peak, narrowing the gap — but platform migration costs still dominate the calculation for existing system owners.

DDR4 vs DDR5 Price-Per-GB in 2026

After the 7.2% DDR5 price drop in late March 2026, the premium over DDR4 has narrowed substantially. DDR5-6000 32 GB kits now start around $80–120, compared to DDR4-3200 32 GB at $50–70. The per-GB premium for DDR5 has fallen from the 3x range at launch to roughly 1.4–1.7x for equivalent capacity.

For new platform builds, DDR5 represents better long-term value: DDR4 production is winding down at major fabs, meaning DDR4 will not continue to fall in price significantly. Analysts at DRAMeXchange project DDR5 will reach price parity with DDR4 by late 2026 or early 2027. For existing DDR4 system owners, upgrading within the DDR4 ecosystem — for example from DDR4-3200 to DDR4-3600 — often costs under $30 and can deliver a meaningful latency improvement without any platform change.
