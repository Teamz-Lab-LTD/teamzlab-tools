What Is Wet Bulb Temperature?

The wet bulb temperature is the lowest temperature that air can be cooled to through the evaporation of water at constant pressure. It is measured by wrapping a wet cloth around a thermometer bulb and allowing air to flow over it. As water evaporates from the cloth, it absorbs heat and cools the thermometer. The wet bulb temperature is always equal to or lower than the dry bulb (regular) temperature because evaporation always produces cooling. The difference between dry bulb and wet bulb temperatures indicates how much moisture is in the air — a large difference means dry air with high evaporative cooling potential, while a small difference indicates saturated air with limited cooling capacity.

Why Wet Bulb Temperature Matters for Human Survival

Wet bulb temperature has gained attention as a critical metric for human heat tolerance. The human body cools itself primarily through sweat evaporation. When the wet bulb temperature approaches 95 degrees Fahrenheit (35 degrees Celsius), the air is so saturated with moisture that sweat can no longer evaporate from skin, making it impossible for the body to shed metabolic heat. At this threshold, even a healthy person resting in the shade will develop fatal hyperthermia within approximately six hours. While wet bulb temperatures of 95 degrees are extremely rare today, climate change is causing them to occur more frequently in regions like the Persian Gulf, South Asia, and parts of the central United States.

Wet Bulb Danger Levels and Work Guidelines

Occupational safety organizations worldwide use wet bulb temperature or the related Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) to set work/rest schedules for outdoor laborers. Below 80 degrees Fahrenheit wet bulb, conditions are generally safe for most activities with normal hydration. Between 80 and 85 degrees, caution is warranted and light work should include regular rest breaks. Between 85 and 88 degrees, strenuous activity should be significantly reduced, with a 15-minute rest period every 45 minutes. Above 88 degrees, only essential light work should be performed with frequent rest in cool areas. Above 95 degrees, human survival is at risk regardless of fitness or acclimatization. These thresholds form the basis of OSHA heat illness prevention guidelines and military training doctrine.

Wet Bulb Temperature in Sports and Athletics

Sports organizations use wet bulb temperature to make practice and game decisions that protect athletes from heat illness. The American College of Sports Medicine recommends modifying or canceling outdoor practices when the wet bulb temperature exceeds certain thresholds. Football, soccer, and other high-intensity sports are particularly affected because athletes wearing equipment generate more heat and have less skin area available for evaporative cooling. Many high school athletic associations now require mandatory wet bulb temperature measurements before outdoor practices during hot weather months. Coaches and athletic trainers use these readings to adjust practice duration, intensity, equipment requirements, and water break frequency.

Climate Change and Rising Wet Bulb Temperatures

Climate research shows that wet bulb temperatures are increasing globally as rising air temperatures combine with higher atmospheric moisture content. Regions that are already hot and humid, such as the Persian Gulf, the Indo-Gangetic Plain, and equatorial coastal areas, are approaching human tolerance limits more frequently. Studies project that by 2050, billions of people may regularly experience wet bulb temperatures that exceed safe thresholds for outdoor work. This has profound implications for agriculture, construction, military operations, and daily life in tropical and subtropical regions. Understanding wet bulb temperature is increasingly important for public health planning, urban design, and climate adaptation strategies.

Differences Between Wet Bulb, Heat Index, and WBGT

While wet bulb temperature, heat index, and Wet Bulb Globe Temperature are all measures of heat stress, they differ in what they incorporate. Wet bulb temperature accounts for air temperature and humidity only. Heat index uses the same inputs but produces a value in familiar temperature units that represents how hot it feels to the human body. WBGT is the most comprehensive measure, incorporating dry bulb temperature, wet bulb temperature, and black globe temperature (which measures radiant heat from sunlight). WBGT is the gold standard for occupational heat stress assessment but requires specialized equipment. This calculator provides the wet bulb temperature component, which is the most important factor in WBGT and sufficient for most practical heat safety decisions.
