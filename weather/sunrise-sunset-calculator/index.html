How Sunrise and Sunset Times Are Calculated

Sunrise and sunset times depend on your geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude), the date, and the tilt of Earth's axis relative to its orbit around the Sun. This calculator uses a simplified version of the NOAA solar position algorithm, which computes the Julian day number, the solar mean anomaly, the equation of center, the ecliptic longitude of the Sun, the Sun's declination, and the hour angle at the horizon. From these astronomical parameters, it derives the precise moment when the center of the Sun crosses the horizon for any point on Earth. The results are accurate to within approximately one to two minutes for most populated latitudes.

Understanding Solar Noon and Day Length

Solar noon is the moment when the Sun reaches its highest point in the sky at your location. This is not always 12:00 PM on the clock because of time zone boundaries and the equation of time, which accounts for irregularities in Earth's orbit and axial tilt. Day length is the total time between sunrise and sunset. At the equator, day length is approximately 12 hours year-round. At higher latitudes, day length varies dramatically with the seasons, ranging from very short winter days to summer days that may include 18 or more hours of sunlight. Near the poles, the Sun may not set at all during summer solstice or rise during winter solstice.

Golden Hour for Photography

The golden hour is the period shortly after sunrise and before sunset when sunlight travels through more atmosphere, producing a warm, diffused, golden light that is highly prized by photographers, filmmakers, and visual artists. This light creates soft shadows, warm tones, and a pleasing glow that is impossible to replicate with artificial lighting. The golden hour typically lasts about one hour after sunrise and one hour before sunset, though the exact duration varies by latitude and time of year. At higher latitudes, the golden hour lasts longer because the Sun crosses the horizon at a shallower angle. Professional photographers plan shoots around golden hour to achieve the most flattering natural light.

Civil Twilight Explained

Civil twilight is the period when the Sun is between the horizon and 6 degrees below it. During morning civil twilight (before sunrise), there is enough natural light for most outdoor activities without artificial lighting. During evening civil twilight (after sunset), light gradually fades until it becomes too dark for normal outdoor activities. Civil twilight is important for aviation, where it defines when pilots need instrument lighting and navigation aids. It also matters for outdoor event planning, construction scheduling, and any activity that depends on natural light. The duration of civil twilight increases with latitude and is longest near the solstices at high latitudes.

Factors That Affect Sunrise and Sunset Times

Several factors influence the exact time of sunrise and sunset at your location. Latitude is the primary factor, with locations closer to the equator having more consistent day lengths year-round. Altitude matters because the Sun appears earlier when you are at a higher elevation. Atmospheric refraction bends sunlight around the curve of the Earth, making the Sun visible slightly before it geometrically rises above the horizon and slightly after it sets. This refraction adds about two minutes to the visible day. Local topography such as mountains, hills, and buildings can block the horizon and delay sunrise or advance sunset at ground level.

Practical Applications of Sunrise and Sunset Data

Beyond photography, sunrise and sunset times serve many practical purposes. Farmers use them to plan field work and animal management. Solar energy installers calculate optimal panel angles based on sun position throughout the year. Outdoor event planners schedule activities around available daylight. Hikers and campers use sunset time to ensure they return to camp before dark. Religious observances in many traditions are tied to sunrise or sunset. Astronomers need sunset time to plan telescope observations. Real estate professionals consider sun exposure when evaluating properties. Understanding when the Sun rises and sets helps you plan your day more effectively and safely.
