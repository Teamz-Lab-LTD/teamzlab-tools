Canada Federal Tax Brackets for 2025

Canada uses a progressive federal income tax system with five brackets for 2025. After subtracting the federal Basic Personal Amount of $16,129, CPP employee premiums, EI premiums, and any RRSP deductions from your gross income, the remaining taxable income is taxed at the following graduated rates:

  • 15% — on the first $57,375 of taxable income
  • 20.5% — on income from $57,376 to $114,750
  • 26% — on income from $114,751 to $158,519
  • 29% — on income from $158,520 to $220,000
  • 33% — on income above $220,000

For 2025, Canadian employees can also deduct their CPP contribution (up to $4,034.10) and EI premium (up to $1,077.48) as non-refundable federal tax credits. These payroll deductions effectively reduce the tax you owe on top of the bracket calculation. Because income is taxed in layers — not all at one flat rate — a person earning $100,000 does not pay 26% on the entire $100,000. Only the portion above each threshold is taxed at the higher rate.

Provincial Income Tax: Ontario, BC, Alberta, and Beyond

Every province and territory levies its own income tax on top of federal tax. Provincial rates and brackets are separate from federal brackets and vary significantly. Alberta is notably the lowest-tax province, with a 10% flat rate on the first $148,269 of income. Quebec has the highest combined rates for most income levels. Here is a simplified overview of the top six provinces covered in this calculator for 2025:

  • Ontario: 5.05% to $51,446 → 9.15% to $102,894 → 11.16% to $150,000 → 12.16% to $220,000 → 13.16% above. Provincial basic personal amount: $11,865.
  • British Columbia: 5.06% to $47,937 → 7.70% to $95,875 → 10.50% to $110,076 → 12.29% to $133,664 → 14.70% to $181,232 → 16.80% above. Provincial BPA: $11,981.
  • Alberta: 10% flat to $148,269 → 12% to $177,922 → 13% to $237,230 → 14% to $355,845 → 15% above. Provincial BPA: $21,997 (highest in Canada).
  • Quebec: 14% to $51,780 → 19% to $103,545 → 24% to $126,000 → 25.75% above. Provincial BPA: $17,183. Quebec administers its own tax separately through Revenu Quebec.
  • Manitoba: 10.8% to $47,000 → 12.75% to $100,000 → 17.4% above. Provincial BPA: $15,780.
  • Saskatchewan: 10.5% to $49,720 → 12.5% to $142,058 → 14.5% above. Provincial BPA: $17,661.

If you live in a territory (Yukon, NWT, Nunavut) or a province not listed above (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, PEI, Newfoundland), select "Other" and the calculator will provide a federal-only estimate. Each of these jurisdictions has unique brackets that change annually — always verify with CRA's official publications for your filing year.

How RRSP Contributions Reduce Your Canadian Tax Bill

An RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan) contribution is one of the most powerful tax-reduction strategies available to Canadians. Every dollar you contribute to your RRSP is subtracted from your gross income before both federal and provincial tax is calculated. This means the tax savings compound across both levels of government. At a combined marginal rate of 43% (federal + provincial), contributing $15,000 to your RRSP saves you $6,450 in taxes — effectively meaning the government funds nearly half your retirement savings.

For 2025, the RRSP contribution room is 18% of your 2024 earned income, up to approximately $31,560 (the 2025 annual dollar limit). Unused room carries forward indefinitely. The contribution deadline for the 2024 tax year is March 1, 2025. In this calculator, enter your RRSP contributions for the current filing year to see exactly how much they reduce your tax payable. Self-employed Canadians benefit especially from RRSP contributions since they typically face higher combined CPP costs — using RRSP deductions to drop a bracket can result in substantial savings.

CPP and EI: What Employees and Self-Employed Pay in 2025

In addition to income tax, most Canadian workers contribute to the Canada Pension Plan (CPP) and Employment Insurance (EI). For 2025:

  • CPP (Employee): 5.95% on earnings between $3,500 and $71,300, up to a maximum of $4,034.10 per year.
  • EI (Employee): 1.66% on insurable earnings up to $64,900, up to a maximum of $1,077.48 per year.
  • CPP (Self-Employed): 11.9% on net self-employment earnings between $3,500 and $71,300, up to $8,068.20 per year (both employee and employer shares combined).
  • Self-employed individuals do not pay EI premiums and are not eligible for regular EI benefits (unless they opt in voluntarily).

CPP contributions qualify as a non-refundable federal and provincial tax credit, partially offsetting their cost. EI premiums are also tax-deductible as a federal non-refundable credit. This calculator applies these credits automatically in the federal tax computation.
