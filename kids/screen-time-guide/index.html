Screen Time Guidelines by Age

The World Health Organization (WHO) and the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) have established evidence-based screen time recommendations for children. These guidelines are based on extensive research into the effects of screen exposure on developing brains, physical health, and social-emotional well-being. The recommendations differ by age group because the risks and benefits of screen time change significantly as children mature.

For children under 2 years, the recommendation is zero screen time except for video calls with family. Infants and toddlers learn primarily through hands-on exploration and face-to-face interaction. Screens cannot replicate the reciprocal communication that builds neural pathways during this critical developmental window. For ages 2-5, a maximum of one hour per day of high-quality programming is recommended, ideally co-viewed with a parent who can help the child understand and apply what they see.

Recommended Screen Time Limits

Under 2 years: No screen time (except video calls)

Ages 2-5: Maximum 1 hour per day

Ages 6-12: Maximum 2 hours per day (recreational)

Ages 13-17: Consistent limits, prioritize sleep and activity

Source: WHO (2019) and American Academy of Pediatrics guidelines.

How Excess Screen Time Affects Children

Research consistently shows that excessive screen time is associated with negative outcomes across multiple domains of child development. Physical health effects include increased risk of obesity (due to sedentary behavior and snacking while watching), disrupted sleep patterns (especially from blue light exposure before bedtime), and delayed motor development in young children who spend time watching instead of crawling, climbing, and playing.

Cognitive and academic effects include reduced attention spans, lower language development in toddlers exposed to background television, and decreased academic performance in school-age children. The AAP notes that for every hour of screen time, young children hear fewer words from caregivers and engage in less imaginative play. Social-emotional effects include increased anxiety and depression symptoms in teens with high social media use, cyberbullying exposure, and reduced face-to-face social skills development.

Not All Screen Time Is Equal

Context matters significantly when evaluating screen time. Educational programming on platforms like PBS Kids, interactive learning apps, and video calls with grandparents have different effects than passive consumption of fast-paced cartoons or endless scrolling through social media. Co-viewing, where a parent watches and discusses content with the child, transforms passive screen time into an interactive learning experience. Creative screen activities like drawing on tablets, making music, or learning to code also differ from purely consumptive use.

Building Healthy Screen Habits

Rather than viewing screen time as purely negative, the goal is to help children develop a healthy relationship with technology. Establish screen-free zones (bedrooms, dining table) and screen-free times (the hour before bed, during meals). Create a family media plan that balances screen activities with physical play, reading, creative activities, and social interaction. Lead by example, as children model their parents' screen habits. Use parental controls and content filters appropriate for your child's age, and regularly discuss online safety.

Alternative Activities by Age Group

Replacing screen time with enriching alternatives supports healthy development. For toddlers (1-3 years), offer building blocks, sensory play, picture books, outdoor exploration, and imaginative play with dolls or vehicles. For preschoolers (3-5 years), try arts and crafts, puzzles, bike riding, playground visits, and pretend play. School-age children (6-12) benefit from sports, musical instruments, board games, cooking together, gardening, and reading. Teenagers (13-17) thrive with team sports, creative hobbies, volunteering, part-time jobs, and in-person social activities.

The Sleep Connection

Screen time in the hour before bed is particularly harmful because blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production, making it harder to fall asleep. Studies show that children who use screens before bed take longer to fall asleep, sleep fewer total hours, and have poorer sleep quality. Given that adequate sleep is critical for growth, learning, emotional regulation, and immune function, establishing a screen-free bedtime routine is one of the most impactful changes families can make. Replace pre-bed screens with books, quiet conversation, or relaxation activities.
