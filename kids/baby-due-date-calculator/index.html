How Pregnancy Due Dates Are Calculated

A pregnancy due date, also called the estimated date of delivery (EDD), is the approximate date when your baby is expected to arrive. The most common method used by doctors and midwives worldwide is Naegele's Rule, which calculates the due date as 280 days (40 weeks) from the first day of your last menstrual period. This method assumes a 28-day menstrual cycle and that ovulation occurred on day 14. While only about 5% of babies are born exactly on their due date, approximately 80% arrive within two weeks before or after.

Due Date Formulas

LMP Method: Due Date = LMP + 280 days + (Cycle Length - 28) days

Conception Date: Due Date = Conception + 266 days

IVF 3-Day Transfer: Due Date = Transfer + 263 days

IVF 5-Day Transfer: Due Date = Transfer + 261 days

Pregnancy lasts approximately 40 weeks from LMP or 38 weeks from conception.

Understanding the Three Trimesters

Pregnancy is divided into three trimesters, each lasting roughly 13 weeks. The first trimester (weeks 1-12) is a period of rapid cell division and organ formation. During this time, the embryo develops a heartbeat, brain, spinal cord, and all major organs. Most miscarriages occur during this period, which is why many parents wait until the end of the first trimester to share their news. Common symptoms include morning sickness, fatigue, and breast tenderness.

The second trimester (weeks 13-27) is often called the "golden period" because many early symptoms subside. The baby grows significantly, developing fingerprints, eyebrows, and the ability to hear sounds. The anatomy scan at around 20 weeks provides a detailed look at the baby's development and is when many parents learn the sex. The mother typically begins to feel the baby's movements (quickening) between weeks 16 and 25.

The third trimester (weeks 28-40) focuses on the baby's growth and maturation. The lungs develop surfactant needed for breathing, the brain grows rapidly, and the baby gains weight. The mother may experience Braxton Hicks contractions, back pain, and difficulty sleeping. Full-term is reached at 37 weeks, and labor can begin naturally anytime from then until 42 weeks.

Why Cycle Length Matters

The standard Naegele's Rule assumes a 28-day cycle, but many women have cycles that are shorter or longer. If your cycle is 35 days, ovulation likely occurred around day 21 rather than day 14, meaning conception happened a week later than the standard calculation assumes. This calculator adjusts for cycle length by adding the difference between your actual cycle length and 28 days to the base due date. Women with irregular cycles may find the conception date method more accurate if they tracked ovulation.

Key Pregnancy Milestones

Several important milestones mark a healthy pregnancy journey. The 12-week dating scan confirms the due date and checks for chromosomal abnormalities. The 20-week anatomy scan evaluates the baby's organs, spine, and growth. At 24 weeks, the baby reaches viability, meaning survival outside the womb is possible with intensive medical care. At 37 weeks, the pregnancy is considered full-term. The baby continues to develop brain connections and add fat layers until delivery, which is why doctors advise against elective early delivery unless medically necessary.

IVF Due Date Calculations

For pregnancies conceived through in vitro fertilization, the due date calculation is more precise because the exact date of embryo transfer is known. A 3-day embryo transfer adds 263 days to the transfer date, while a 5-day blastocyst transfer adds 261 days. These calculations account for the age of the embryo at the time of transfer. IVF due dates are generally considered more accurate than LMP-based calculations because they eliminate the uncertainty around ovulation timing.

Accuracy and Limitations

Due date calculations are estimates, not predictions. Ultrasound dating in the first trimester is the most accurate method, typically within 5-7 days. The LMP method can be off by 1-2 weeks if ovulation did not occur on day 14 or if the exact LMP date is uncertain. Factors such as the mother's age, number of previous pregnancies, ethnicity, and individual fetal growth patterns all influence when labor naturally begins. Always discuss your due date with your healthcare provider, who may adjust it based on ultrasound measurements.
