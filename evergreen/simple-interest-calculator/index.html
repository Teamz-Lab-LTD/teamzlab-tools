How Simple Interest Is Calculated

Simple interest is the most straightforward way to calculate the cost of borrowing money or the return on a basic investment. Unlike compound interest, simple interest is always calculated on the original principal — it never grows on itself. The formula is:

Simple Interest (SI) = Principal × Rate × Time / 100

Where Principal (P) is the starting amount, Rate (R) is the annual interest rate as a percentage, and Time (T) is the number of years. The total amount at the end of the period is simply P + SI. For example, a $5,000 loan at 6% per year for 3 years earns $900 in interest, bringing the total to $5,900.

This calculator also breaks down your interest into monthly and daily amounts — useful for understanding the true cost of short-term borrowing or the pace of savings growth.

Simple Interest vs Compound Interest

The key difference between simple and compound interest is what the rate is applied to each period. With simple interest, the rate always applies to the original principal. With compound interest, the rate applies to the principal plus all previously earned interest.

Over short periods (1–2 years), the difference is small. Over long periods, the gap becomes dramatic. A $10,000 investment at 8% over 30 years earns $24,000 in simple interest ($34,000 total), but grows to over $100,000 with annual compounding. For savings and investments, compound interest almost always works in your favour. For loans, simple interest is generally cheaper for the borrower.

Common real-world uses of simple interest include car loans, personal loans, most short-term credit products, US Treasury bills, and some savings certificates. If a lender advertises a flat-rate loan, it is almost certainly using simple interest.

Tips for Borrowers and Savers

When borrowing, the total amount you repay is what matters — not just the rate. A lower rate over a longer term can cost more than a higher rate over a shorter term. Always calculate total interest, not just the monthly payment. When saving, even a small increase in rate has a meaningful impact over several years. A 1% rate increase on a $20,000 deposit held for 5 years adds $1,000 in simple interest — the equivalent of one extra month of returns for free.
