How the Savings Calculator Works

This savings calculator uses the compound interest formula to project your savings growth over time. It accounts for your initial deposit, recurring monthly contributions, the annual interest rate, and how frequently interest compounds. The result shows exactly how much of your final balance comes from your own contributions versus earned interest.

Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)nt + PMT × [((1 + r/n)nt − 1) / (r/n)]

Where: P = initial deposit, r = annual interest rate, n = compounds per year, t = years, PMT = periodic contribution

Monthly compounding generates slightly more interest than annual compounding because earned interest begins earning its own interest sooner. Over long time horizons, this difference becomes significant. For example, $10,000 at 6% compounded monthly for 30 years yields about $1,400 more than annual compounding.

Savings Calculator for Retirement Planning

Planning for retirement starts with understanding how much your savings can grow. A person who starts saving $400 per month at age 25 with a 7% average annual return will accumulate over $1 million by age 65. Starting the same contributions at age 35 yields roughly $480,000 — less than half. The earlier you begin, the more compound interest works in your favor. How Compound Interest Grows Your Money

Compound interest creates exponential growth because each interest payment increases the base for the next calculation. In the first few years, growth appears modest. But over decades, interest-on-interest accelerates dramatically. A $5,000 deposit with $300 monthly contributions at 5% grows to about $52,000 in 10 years, $120,000 in 20 years, and over $220,000 in 30 years. Tips to Maximize Your Savings

Start as early as possible. Even small amounts benefit from decades of compounding. Automate your monthly contributions so you never miss a deposit. Choose accounts with the highest interest rate available — high-yield savings accounts and CDs often offer better rates than traditional banks. Increase contributions whenever your income rises, even by $25 per month. Avoid withdrawing interest; let it compound. Finally, review your savings plan annually and adjust your contributions and rate assumptions to stay on track toward your goals.
