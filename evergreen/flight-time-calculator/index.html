How the Flight Time Calculator Works

A flight time calculator is a free tool that estimates the duration of a flight between two cities using the Haversine formula to compute great circle distance — the shortest path between two points on Earth's surface. It takes the latitude and longitude of both airports, calculates the distance in kilometers and miles, then divides by aircraft cruising speed to estimate flight time. A 30-minute buffer is added for takeoff and landing procedures. The calculator also estimates jet lag based on timezone differences between departure and arrival cities. All calculations run locally in your browser with no data sent to any server.

Understanding Great Circle Distance

Aircraft fly along great circle routes — the shortest path between two points on a sphere. This is why flight paths on flat maps appear curved. A flight from New York to Tokyo follows an arc over the North Pacific rather than a straight line across the map. The Haversine formula calculates this distance using GPS coordinates, producing results accurate to within 0.5% of actual flight distances. Real flight distances may vary slightly due to air traffic control routing, weather diversions, and restricted airspace. Factors That Affect Flight Duration

Several factors cause actual flight times to differ from calculated estimates. Jet streams — high-altitude wind currents flowing west to east at 100-200 mph — make eastbound flights faster and westbound flights slower by 30-60 minutes on long-haul routes. Headwinds and tailwinds at cruising altitude can add or subtract 10-15% from estimated flight time. Air traffic congestion near busy airports adds holding patterns that extend flights by 10-30 minutes. Aircraft type matters too: the average commercial jet cruises at 550 mph, but regional turboprops fly at 300-350 mph. Weather diversions can add significant time to any route.

Managing Jet Lag Across Time Zones

Jet lag occurs when you cross three or more time zones, disrupting your circadian rhythm. The general rule is that your body needs one day to adjust per timezone crossed. Eastward travel is typically harder than westward because it requires advancing your body clock, which is more difficult than delaying it. To minimize jet lag, start adjusting your sleep schedule 2-3 days before departure by shifting bedtime 30-60 minutes toward your destination timezone. Stay hydrated during the flight, avoid excessive caffeine and alcohol, and expose yourself to sunlight at your destination to help reset your internal clock.
