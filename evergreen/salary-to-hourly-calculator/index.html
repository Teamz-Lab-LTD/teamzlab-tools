How the Salary to Hourly Conversion Works

Converting an annual salary to an hourly rate is straightforward: divide the total annual salary by the number of hours worked per year. The number of hours per year is your hours per week multiplied by the number of weeks you work.

Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ (Hours per Week × Weeks per Year)

For a standard full-time employee working 40 hours per week for 52 weeks, the total is 2,080 working hours per year. A $52,000 annual salary therefore equals exactly $25.00 per hour. This calculator makes it easy to adjust both variables — ideal for part-time roles, freelance rates, or contracts with paid leave provisions. From the hourly rate, all other pay periods are calculated: daily (hourly × hours per day), weekly (hourly × weekly hours), biweekly (annual ÷ 26), and monthly (annual ÷ 12).

Why Convert Salary to Hourly Rate?

Knowing your effective hourly rate is valuable in several real-world situations:

Job offer comparison: When evaluating two offers — one salaried and one hourly — converting to a common unit makes fair comparison easy. A $75,000 salary and a $38/hour contract can be compared directly once you account for actual hours worked.

Freelance rate setting: Freelancers and consultants who previously worked as salaried employees often use their last salary as a baseline. Converting to an hourly rate and then adding a premium for self-employment taxes, benefits gaps, and unpaid time gives a fair market freelance rate.

Overtime and pay dispute checks: Salaried employees who become eligible for overtime pay (common in some jurisdictions when reclassified as non-exempt) need to know their hourly equivalent rate to verify correct overtime payments.

Part-time to full-time modelling: If you are considering reducing to 30 hours a week, simply change the hours per week field to see how your equivalent annual salary changes. This helps with budget planning before making the switch.

Gross Pay vs Take-Home Pay

This calculator shows your gross pay — the amount before taxes and deductions are taken out. Your actual take-home (net) pay will be lower after income tax, Social Security, Medicare, state taxes, health insurance premiums, and any pension contributions are deducted.

As a rough guide, most full-time employees in the US take home 65–75% of their gross salary after standard deductions. Higher earners in high-tax states may take home closer to 55–60%. Use the take-home pay estimator linked below for a more precise after-tax figure.
