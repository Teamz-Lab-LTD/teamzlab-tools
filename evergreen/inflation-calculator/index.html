What Is Inflation and How Does It Work?

Inflation is the gradual increase in the price of goods and services over time. When inflation rises, each unit of currency buys fewer items than it did before. Central banks around the world target a moderate inflation rate (usually around 2%) to keep economies growing without prices spiraling out of control.

Inflation Calculator Formula

How CPI Is Calculated

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is the most widely used measure of inflation. Government statistical agencies track the price of a representative basket of goods and services — including food, housing, transportation, healthcare, and education — and compare those prices to a base period. The percentage change in this index from one period to the next is the inflation rate. Different countries use slightly different methodologies, but the core principle remains the same.

Historical Inflation Trends by Country

Inflation rates vary significantly across countries and time periods. The United States has averaged roughly 3.0% annual inflation over the past century, while India has experienced closer to 6.0% on average. The UK sits around 3.2%, Canada at 2.8%, Australia at 3.5%, and the Eurozone at 2.5%. These averages smooth out periods of extreme volatility — the 1970s oil crisis pushed US inflation above 13%, while the 2010s saw historically low rates below 2%.

How Inflation Affects Your Money

Inflation silently erodes your purchasing power. If you keep $10,000 under your mattress for 20 years at 3% average inflation, that money would only buy roughly $5,500 worth of goods in today's terms. This is why financial advisors stress the importance of investing rather than simply saving. Any return below the inflation rate means you are effectively losing money in real terms.

Tips to Beat Inflation

To protect your wealth from inflation, consider diversifying your investments across asset classes that historically outpace rising prices. Stocks have returned roughly 7-10% annually over long periods, well above inflation. Real estate provides both rental income and property appreciation. Government inflation-protected securities (like US TIPS or UK index-linked gilts) adjust their principal with inflation. Even high-yield savings accounts can help in moderate inflation environments. The key is to never leave large sums idle in accounts earning zero or near-zero interest.
