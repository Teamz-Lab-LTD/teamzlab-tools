How the Capacitor Calculator Works

This capacitor calculator computes the total capacitance of series and parallel circuits, energy stored in a capacitor, charge on the plates, RC time constant, and capacitive reactance at a given frequency. All calculations run instantly in your browser with no data sent to any server. Enter your capacitance values with the appropriate unit and the calculator applies standard EEE formulas. In a parallel circuit, total capacitance is the sum of all individual capacitors. In a series circuit, the reciprocal of the total capacitance equals the sum of reciprocals of each capacitor. The energy stored in a capacitor is given by E equals one-half times capacitance times voltage squared, while charge is simply capacitance times voltage. The RC time constant represents the time for a capacitor to charge to approximately 63.2 percent of the supply voltage through a resistor. Capacitive reactance describes how a capacitor opposes alternating current at a specific frequency.

Series vs Parallel Capacitors

Understanding series and parallel capacitor configurations is fundamental to circuit design. When capacitors are connected in parallel, their capacitances add directly because each capacitor sees the full voltage across its terminals. This configuration is used to increase total capacitance or to combine standard values to reach a specific target value. When capacitors are connected in series, the total capacitance decreases — the resulting capacitance is always less than the smallest individual capacitor. Series connections are used to increase the voltage rating of the combination or to achieve small capacitance values not available as standard components. In practice, many circuits use combinations of series and parallel capacitors to meet both capacitance and voltage requirements. Decoupling capacitors on power supply rails are typically placed in parallel, while capacitors in voltage multiplier circuits are arranged in series.

Capacitor Applications in EEE

Capacitors are among the most widely used passive components in electrical and electronic engineering. In power supply circuits, large electrolytic capacitors smooth rectified AC into stable DC voltage. Ceramic capacitors provide high-frequency decoupling on integrated circuit power pins, filtering noise that could cause digital logic errors. In timing circuits, the RC time constant determines oscillation frequency, delay intervals, and filter cutoff points. Audio crossover networks use capacitors to separate frequency bands for tweeters and woofers. In motor start circuits, capacitors provide the phase shift needed to create a rotating magnetic field in single-phase induction motors. Power factor correction capacitors reduce reactive power in industrial installations, lowering electricity costs and reducing strain on distribution networks. Energy storage capacitors — especially supercapacitors — are increasingly used in regenerative braking systems and backup power supplies where rapid charge and discharge cycles are required.

Capacitance Unit Conversion Guide

Capacitance is measured in farads (F), but one farad is an extremely large value for most applications. Common subunits include picofarads (pF, 10-12 F), nanofarads (nF, 10-9 F), microfarads (uF, 10-6 F), and millifarads (mF, 10-3 F). Ceramic capacitors are typically rated in picofarads or nanofarads, while electrolytic and film capacitors use microfarads. Supercapacitors can reach hundreds or thousands of farads.
