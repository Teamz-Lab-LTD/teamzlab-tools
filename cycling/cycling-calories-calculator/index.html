How Cycling Burns Calories

Calorie burn while cycling is calculated using the MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) method, a scientifically validated approach used in sports medicine. The formula is: Calories = MET × weight in kg × duration in hours. MET values range from 4.0 for leisurely rides under 16 km/h up to 12.0 or higher for competitive racing speeds above 26 km/h. This calculator applies an additional terrain multiplier — flat roads have no adjustment, mixed terrain adds 15%, and hilly terrain adds 30% — to reflect the extra muscular effort required for climbing.

Unlike running, where calorie burn per kilometre stays relatively constant, cycling calorie burn is highly sensitive to speed because aerodynamic drag increases with the square of velocity. Doubling your speed from 15 km/h to 30 km/h does not merely double the effort — it roughly quadruples the power required to overcome wind resistance. This is why racing cyclists burn dramatically more calories than casual commuters even over the same distance.

Calories Burned by Speed and Terrain

Speed and terrain are the two biggest variables in cycling calorie expenditure. For a 70 kg rider cycling for one hour:

  • Light pace (<16 km/h, MET 4.0): approximately 280 calories on flat roads
  • Moderate pace (16-19 km/h, MET 6.8): approximately 476 calories on flat roads
  • Vigorous pace (19-22 km/h, MET 8.0): approximately 560 calories on flat roads
  • Very vigorous (22-26 km/h, MET 10.0): approximately 700 calories on flat roads
  • Racing (>26 km/h, MET 12.0): approximately 840 calories on flat roads

Add hilly terrain and these figures rise by 30%, making a vigorous hilly ride burn close to 730 calories per hour for a 70 kg cyclist. Headwinds have a similar effect — a strong headwind at 30 km/h can increase your effective effort by 50% or more, dramatically increasing calorie burn without any change in perceived speed over the ground.

Cycling vs Running vs Walking

Per hour, vigorous cycling (MET 8.0) burns a similar number of calories as running at a moderate jog (MET 8.3). However, because cycling is low-impact, most people can sustain cycling for much longer periods — a two-hour bike ride burns far more total calories than a 30-minute run even if the per-hour rate is similar. Compared to brisk walking (MET 3.5), moderate cycling burns almost double the calories per hour. The table produced by this calculator shows the comparison side by side using your own body weight, so you can plan which activity best fits your fitness goal and available time.

Cycling also has a notable advantage for calorie burning: it can be incorporated into daily commuting. A 30-minute bike commute each way adds up to five hours of moderate exercise per week — burning an extra 2,000-3,500 calories without requiring dedicated gym time.

Tips to Maximize Calorie Burn While Cycling

If your goal is maximum calorie burn, consider these evidence-backed strategies:

  • Interval training: Alternate 30-60 seconds of sprint effort with 2-3 minutes of easy riding. HIIT cycling burns 25-35% more calories than steady-state rides of the same duration.
  • Choose hilly routes: Climbing forces your largest muscle groups (glutes, quads, hamstrings) to work at high intensity, significantly raising your heart rate and MET.
  • Increase resistance: On an indoor bike, adding resistance simulates hills and increases calorie burn without requiring higher speed.
  • Ride in the morning: Some studies suggest fasted morning cardio (before breakfast) increases fat oxidation during exercise.
  • Stay consistent: The fitter you become, the more efficiently your body moves — you may need to gradually increase duration or intensity to maintain the same calorie deficit.

Cycling for Weight Loss

To lose 0.5 kg per week, you need a deficit of approximately 3,850 calories per week (roughly 550 calories per day). Cycling 60 minutes at moderate pace burns 400-550 calories for an average-weight adult, making it one of the most practical daily exercises for sustainable weight loss. Combined with a modest dietary adjustment — reducing portion sizes rather than extreme restriction — cycling five days per week can reliably produce 0.5-1 kg of fat loss per week without the joint stress associated with running.

Key insight: fat burned per session depends on calorie deficit, not just calories burned during exercise. After a vigorous 60-minute ride burning 560 calories, your metabolic rate stays elevated for 30-60 minutes (the afterburn effect), adding an extra 30-80 calories. Consistency matters more than intensity — three moderate rides per week sustained over months will always outperform sporadic intense sessions.
