How It Works

The Late Fee Calculator helps you determine the exact penalty amount owed on an overdue invoice. Whether you charge a flat fee, a monthly percentage, or a daily percentage on late payments, this tool computes the fee based on the number of days the invoice is past due and the original invoice amount. It also calculates the total amount due including the late fee so you can include accurate figures in your collection notices and follow-up communications.

Late fees serve an important purpose in business: they incentivize timely payment, compensate the creditor for the cost of carrying unpaid receivables, and discourage habitual late payers. Including a clear late fee policy in your contracts and invoices is a widely accepted business practice and is legal in most jurisdictions, provided the fees are reasonable and disclosed upfront.

Flat Fee Formula

Late Fee = Fixed Amount (regardless of days overdue)

Total Due = Invoice Amount + Flat Fee

Monthly Percentage Formula

Months Overdue = Days Overdue / 30

Late Fee = Invoice Amount × (Monthly Rate / 100) × Months Overdue

Total Due = Invoice Amount + Late Fee

Daily Percentage Formula

Late Fee = Invoice Amount × (Daily Rate / 100) × Days Overdue

Total Due = Invoice Amount + Late Fee

Understanding Late Fee Types

There are three common approaches to calculating late fees, each with its own advantages depending on your business model and the nature of your client relationships.

Flat Fee

A flat fee is a fixed penalty amount charged once an invoice becomes overdue, regardless of how many days it remains unpaid. For example, you might charge a $50 late fee on any invoice that is not paid by the due date. This approach is simple to understand, easy to communicate, and straightforward to enforce. However, it does not scale with the invoice amount, which means a $50 fee on a $500 invoice (10%) is proportionally much higher than a $50 fee on a $50,000 invoice (0.1%). Flat fees work best for businesses with consistent invoice sizes or for smaller invoices where simplicity is valued.

Monthly Percentage

A monthly percentage fee is calculated based on the outstanding invoice amount and the number of months (or fractions of months) the payment is overdue. The most common monthly rate is 1.5%, which translates to an annual rate of 18%. This method scales proportionally with both the invoice size and the duration of the delay, making it fair for both small and large invoices. It is the most commonly used late fee structure in B2B contracts and is the default recommendation for most businesses.

Daily Percentage

A daily percentage fee applies a small rate for each calendar day the invoice is overdue. This is the most granular approach and creates a strong incentive for the client to pay as soon as possible, since the fee increases every day. A typical daily rate might be 0.05% (which equals roughly 1.5% per month or 18.25% annually). Daily rates are commonly used in industries like logistics, wholesale, and financial services where precise interest accrual is standard practice.

Examples

Example 1: Monthly Percentage

Invoice amount: $5,000. Due date: January 15. Payment received: February 14 (30 days late). Monthly rate: 1.5%. Late fee = $5,000 × 1.5% × (30/30) = $75.00. Total due: $5,075.00.

Example 2: Flat Fee

Invoice amount: $2,500. Due date: March 1. Payment received: March 20 (19 days late). Flat fee: $50. Total due: $2,550.00. The flat fee applies regardless of whether payment is 1 day or 60 days late.

Example 3: Daily Percentage

Invoice amount: $10,000. Due date: June 1. Payment received: June 16 (15 days late). Daily rate: 0.05%. Late fee = $10,000 × 0.05% × 15 = $75.00. Total due: $10,075.00.

Legal Considerations

Late fees are legal in most jurisdictions, but there are important guidelines to follow. The fee must be disclosed in advance, typically in the contract or on the invoice itself. Many states and countries have usury laws that cap the maximum interest rate you can charge, so be sure to check your local regulations. As a general rule, late fees should be reasonable and proportional to the amount owed. Courts may refuse to enforce penalties that are deemed excessive or punitive. Including a clear late fee clause in your terms of service or contract is the best way to ensure enforceability and avoid disputes.
