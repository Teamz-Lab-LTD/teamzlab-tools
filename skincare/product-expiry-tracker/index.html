Why Skincare Product Expiry Dates Matter

Using expired skincare products is more than just wasting money — it can actively harm your skin. When a product passes its expiry date or PAO period, the preservative system that keeps bacteria and mould at bay begins to fail. This means you may be applying a product that harbours harmful microorganisms directly onto your face. For people with sensitive or acne-prone skin, expired products are a common but overlooked trigger for sudden breakouts, redness, or irritation.

Active ingredients also degrade significantly once a product is opened and exposed to oxygen, light, and moisture. Vitamin C, retinol, AHAs, and BHAs all lose potency over time. If you wonder why your once-effective serum suddenly stopped working, product age is often the answer. Swapping an oxidised vitamin C serum for a fresh one can make a noticeable difference in brightness and glow.

Beyond efficacy, there is a safety concern with SPF products. Expired sunscreen may offer far less than the labelled protection — meaning you think you are at SPF 50 but your real-world protection could be closer to SPF 10 or lower. This matters especially in summer or high UV environments where adequate protection prevents long-term skin damage.

Understanding PAO: Period After Opening Explained

The PAO symbol is an internationally standardised icon that appears on almost every cosmetic product manufactured in the EU and widely adopted worldwide. It looks like a small open jar with a number and the letter M inside — for example, "12M" means the product is safe to use for 12 months after you first open it.

The PAO is different from the printed best-before date stamped on the packaging. The best-before date assumes the product stays sealed and stored correctly. Once you open the packaging, the clock starts on the PAO period — and that countdown is usually much shorter. A product might have a best-before date three years from manufacture, but once opened, the PAO might be only 6 or 12 months.

When both dates exist, always respect whichever one comes first. If a product's printed expiry date is next month but you only opened it two weeks ago, the printed date takes priority. Conversely, if you opened a sunscreen 14 months ago and the PAO is 12M, it has expired regardless of what is printed on the tube.

Which Skincare Products Expire Fastest?

Vitamin C serums are among the most unstable skincare products. L-Ascorbic Acid, the most potent form of vitamin C, oxidises rapidly on contact with air and light. Most vitamin C serums have a PAO of just 3 to 6 months. You can spot an oxidised vitamin C serum easily — it will turn from its original pale or clear colour to yellow, then orange, then brown. Once it darkens significantly, the active ingredient has degraded and should be replaced.

Retinol and other retinoids also degrade over time. Retinol is sensitive to light and oxygen, which is why most retinol products come in opaque or amber packaging. Once opened, use within 6 to 12 months for best results. Storing your retinol in a cool, dark place helps extend its potency within the PAO window.

Mascaras, although not skincare, are worth noting — they have a PAO of just 3 months due to the wet, dark tube being ideal for bacterial growth close to the eyes. For skincare specifically, exfoliants (AHAs and BHAs) should be used within 12 months as their acid potency diminishes. Cleansers and moisturisers without active ingredients are generally the most stable, with PAOs of 12 to 24 months.
