How Fantasy Football Scoring Works

Fantasy football scoring converts real NFL player statistics into points for your fantasy team. Every passing yard, rushing touchdown, reception, and turnover translates into a specific point value. The three main scoring formats are Standard (no points for receptions), PPR (1 point per reception), and Half-PPR (0.5 points per reception). Understanding these formats is essential for drafting the right players and making smart lineup decisions each week.

In Standard scoring, running backs who get most of their value from rushing are favored. In PPR formats, pass-catching running backs and high-volume wide receivers gain a significant edge because every catch adds a full point. Half-PPR strikes a balance, rewarding receptions without making them overly dominant. Most modern fantasy leagues use PPR or Half-PPR scoring because it adds another dimension to player evaluation and reduces the gap between positions.

PPR vs Standard: Which Format Is Better?

The debate between PPR and Standard scoring comes down to how much you value receptions. In PPR leagues, a running back who catches 5 passes for 30 yards scores 8 points from receiving alone (5 receptions + 3 yards points). In Standard, that same line scores only 3 points. This dramatically changes player rankings and draft strategy. Wide receivers and pass-catching tight ends become more valuable in PPR, while power rushers who rarely catch passes lose relative value.

Half-PPR has emerged as the most popular compromise. It rewards receptions enough to differentiate players but does not make volume receivers disproportionately valuable. When comparing players across formats, always check how many receptions they average per game. A player who catches 6 passes per game gains 6 extra points in PPR, 3 in Half-PPR, and 0 in Standard, which can be the difference between a starter and a bench player.

Draft Strategy and Scoring Optimization

Your scoring format should directly influence your draft strategy. In PPR leagues, target wide receivers and tight ends with high reception counts early. Players like slot receivers who see 8-10 targets per game become league-winners in PPR because their floor is elevated by volume. In Standard leagues, prioritize running backs who dominate carries and score rushing touchdowns, since they do not benefit from the reception bonus.

Quarterbacks are scored differently from other positions. Passing yards are worth only 0.04 points (25 yards per point) compared to 0.1 points per rushing or receiving yard (10 yards per point). This is why a QB who throws for 300 yards gets 12 passing points, while a running back who rushes for 100 yards gets 10 points from a much smaller yardage total. Touchdowns are the great equalizer: passing TDs are worth 4 points while rushing and receiving TDs are worth 6. Understanding these values helps you project weekly scores and identify mismatches in your lineup.
