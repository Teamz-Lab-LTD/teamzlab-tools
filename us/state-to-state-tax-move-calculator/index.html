Why Remote Workers Are Moving to No-Income-Tax States

Nine US states impose zero state income tax on wages: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (no wage tax), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. A remote worker earning $150,000 in California pays roughly $12,500 in state income tax. The same worker living in Texas or Florida pays $0 — a difference of $12,500 per year in pure state tax savings, before considering any differences in cost of living. Over 20 years at a modest 3% annual salary growth, that's over $335,000 in lifetime state tax savings from the move alone. High-tax states like California (top rate 13.3%), New York (10.9%), New Jersey (10.75%), and Hawaii (11%) are losing residents to Texas, Florida, Tennessee, and Nevada at record rates.

How State Income Tax Works

State income tax is levied on your earnings in addition to federal tax. Most states use progressive brackets similar to the federal system, with top rates ranging from 0% (nine states) to 13.3% (California). A few states use flat rates: Colorado 4.40%, Illinois 4.95%, Utah 4.55%, and North Carolina 4.25%. Your state tax is based on where you reside and where the work is performed, not where your employer is located. Remote workers who physically relocate while keeping the same remote employer typically shift their state tax liability to the new state after establishing residency (usually 183+ days plus other ties like driver's license, voter registration, and homestead).

Beyond Income Tax: The Real Cost Picture

State income tax is only one piece of the overall tax burden. No-income-tax states often offset revenue through higher sales tax (Tennessee 9.5% average combined), property tax (Texas averages 1.6% of home value vs California's 0.75%), and excise taxes. Florida has no income tax but high homeowner insurance costs from hurricane exposure. New Hampshire has no wage tax but heavy property tax. Cost of living differences matter too — moving from San Francisco to Austin saves income tax but Austin housing costs have risen sharply. This calculator shows income tax savings only. Factor in housing, property tax, sales tax, insurance, and lifestyle before deciding.

Establishing Residency for Tax Purposes

Simply buying a vacation home in Florida does not shift your tax residency. States use a multi-factor test: where you spend most of your time (generally 183+ days), where your family lives, where your driver's license and voter registration are, where your primary bank accounts and doctors are, and where you claim homestead exemption. High-tax states like California and New York aggressively audit former residents who claim to have moved. Document your move carefully: sell or rent out your previous primary residence, register vehicles in the new state, file a final part-year return in the old state, and keep receipts showing physical presence in the new state.
