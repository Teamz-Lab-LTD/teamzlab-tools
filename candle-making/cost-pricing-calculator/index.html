How to Price Your Candles

Pricing candles correctly is one of the most common challenges for new candle makers. Price too low and you work for below minimum wage; price too high and you price yourself out of your market. The right price is determined by your actual costs — not by what competitors charge or by instinct. This calculator makes that process systematic by breaking every cost down to the per-candle level before applying your desired markup.

The fundamental formula is: retail price = total material cost per candle × (1 + markup percentage ÷ 100). A 100% markup means you double your cost price — so a candle that costs $4.00 to make retails at $8.00. A 200% markup gives you $12.00. The appropriate markup depends on your sales channel: direct-to-consumer (Etsy, markets, your website) can support 200–400% markup, while wholesale to retailers requires at least 100–200% markup so the retailer has room to apply their own margin.

The most overlooked cost is overhead. This includes electricity for your melting pot and wick trimmer, packaging materials (boxes, tissue paper, stickers), workspace costs, shipping supplies, and your own time. Even if you work from home, your time has real value. If it takes you 45 minutes to produce, cure, label, and pack 12 candles and you value your time at $20/hr, that is $1.25 per candle in labour cost — often more than the cost of the wick and label combined. Include this in your overhead field.

Understanding Candle Production Costs

Wax is typically the largest cost driver. Soy wax costs $3–6 per pound depending on quality and supplier, so an 8 oz candle uses roughly half a pound of wax — $1.50 to $3.00 just in wax. Paraffin is cheaper at $1.50–3.00/lb but carries an environmental perception cost in some markets. Coconut wax is the most expensive at $8–15/lb but commands premium pricing. Beeswax is similarly premium at $7–12/lb.

Fragrance oil is the second largest cost. At 8% fragrance load, an 8 oz soy candle needs approximately 0.64 oz of fragrance oil. If you pay $2.50/oz for fragrance, that is $1.60 per candle. Premium or designer-dupe fragrances can cost $4–8/oz, pushing fragrance cost to $2.56–5.12 per candle. Buying fragrance oil by the pound rather than by the bottle can cut per-ounce costs by 30–50%.

Jars are the most visible component and heavily influence perceived value. A basic straight-sided glass jar costs $0.80–1.20. An amber glass apothecary jar costs $1.50–2.50. A premium tin costs $0.60–1.20. Buying jars by the case (usually 12 or 24 units) reduces per-jar cost significantly. Wick costs are low ($0.10–0.25 each) but wick quality directly affects burn performance — do not cut corners here.

Setting a Profitable Retail Price

Once you know your cost per candle, the recommended minimum retail price for direct sales is 3–4x your material cost (300–400% markup). This accounts for platform fees (Etsy takes 6.5% + listing fees), payment processing (Stripe/PayPal takes 2.9% + $0.30), packaging, and leaves you a healthy margin. For in-person markets, a 200–300% markup is typical and competitive.

For wholesale, use 2x your cost as your wholesale price — this is the minimum that makes wholesale viable. Retailers typically keystone (double) the wholesale price, so a candle you wholesale at $8 will retail in a shop for $16. If your cost is $4 and you wholesale at $8, your gross margin is 50% — viable but slim. Aim to reduce costs over time by buying in larger bulk and streamlining your process, which improves your margin at the same price point.

Effective pricing strategy also involves knowing your market. A farmers' market customer expects to pay $12–18 for an 8 oz candle; a luxury gifting customer expects to pay $25–40. Your fragrance choices, jar selection, and branding should signal the price tier you are targeting. Use this calculator to work backwards from your target retail price to understand what cost reductions you need to hit your margin goals.
