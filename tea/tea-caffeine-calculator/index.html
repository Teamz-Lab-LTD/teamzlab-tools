How the Tea Caffeine Calculator Works

This tea caffeine calculator estimates how much caffeine you consume from tea each day based on four key factors: tea type, cup size, steeping time, and number of infusions. Each tea variety has a distinct caffeine range determined by the plant cultivar, leaf maturity, and processing method. Black tea typically contains 40 to 70 mg per 8-ounce cup, while green tea ranges from 25 to 50 mg. The calculator uses a logarithmic steeping curve because caffeine extraction is not linear: most caffeine dissolves within the first 3 minutes, with diminishing returns after that. Longer steeps release more caffeine but at a decreasing rate.

Cup size matters significantly. A small 150 ml gaiwan produces far less caffeine per serving than a large 473 ml mug, even with the same tea. The calculator scales caffeine proportionally based on the standard 237 ml reference size. Multiple infusions are also factored in: each subsequent steep extracts roughly 50 to 70 percent of the previous infusion's caffeine, meaning your third steep has substantially less than the first.

Tea Caffeine Compared to Coffee and Energy Drinks

Tea generally contains less caffeine than coffee, but the gap varies widely depending on the type. A single cup of matcha contains 60 to 70 mg of caffeine, approaching a shot of espresso at 63 mg. Black tea at 40 to 70 mg per cup is moderate, while herbal teas like chamomile and rooibos contain zero caffeine. Yerba mate, while not a true tea, delivers 40 to 60 mg per serving, comparable to black tea.

Compared to energy drinks, tea is considerably lower in caffeine. A 16-ounce Monster has 160 mg and a Bang contains 300 mg, amounts that would require 3 to 6 cups of black tea to match. However, tea drinkers who consume 5 or more cups daily can easily reach 200 to 350 mg, approaching the FDA recommended maximum of 400 mg. This calculator helps you visualize exactly where your daily tea habit falls relative to that limit.

Steeping Time and Caffeine Extraction

The relationship between steeping time and caffeine extraction follows a logarithmic curve. In the first minute, roughly 50 to 60 percent of available caffeine dissolves into the water. By 3 minutes, about 80 percent has been extracted. After 5 minutes, extraction slows dramatically, with only marginal increases beyond that point. This is why a 10-minute steep does not contain twice the caffeine of a 5-minute steep. Water temperature also plays a role: hotter water extracts caffeine faster. Black and pu-erh teas brewed at near-boiling temperatures extract more caffeine per minute than green or white teas steeped at lower temperatures.

Multiple infusions follow a similar decay pattern. The first infusion extracts the most caffeine. The second infusion typically contains 50 to 70 percent of the first, and the third drops to 30 to 50 percent. Gongfu-style brewing with short, repeated steeps distributes caffeine more evenly across infusions compared to Western-style brewing with longer single steeps. This calculator accounts for all infusions when computing your total daily intake.

Tips for Managing Tea Caffeine Intake

If you want to reduce caffeine from tea without switching to herbal, try these strategies. First, shorten your steep time to 1 to 2 minutes, which can cut caffeine by 30 to 40 percent compared to a 5-minute steep. Second, use cooler water, especially for green and white teas, which extracts less caffeine. Third, try white tea, which has the lowest caffeine among true teas at 15 to 30 mg per cup. Fourth, discard the first short steep (a 30-second rinse) which removes a significant portion of surface caffeine. Fifth, limit your daily cups using this calculator to stay well within the 400 mg FDA guideline.
