How the WiFi Range Calculator Works

The WiFi Range Calculator is a free browser-based tool that estimates your wireless router's coverage area using the Free Space Path Loss (FSPL) formula. This physics-based equation models how radio signals weaken as they travel through space and encounter obstacles. The calculator takes your router's transmit power, antenna gain, operating frequency, and environment conditions to compute the maximum distance at which devices can maintain a reliable connection.

FSPL is expressed as: FSPL (dB) = 20 log10(distance) + 20 log10(frequency) + 20 log10(4 pi / c). By rearranging this formula and subtracting losses from walls, obstacles, and environmental absorption, the tool solves for the maximum distance at two signal strength thresholds: -70 dBm for a good connection suitable for video calls and streaming, and -80 dBm for a usable connection adequate for web browsing and email.

Factors That Affect WiFi Range

Several factors determine how far your WiFi signal can reach. Frequency band is the most significant: 2.4 GHz signals travel roughly twice as far as 5 GHz signals because lower frequencies experience less atmospheric absorption and diffract better around obstacles. Transmit power and antenna gain directly increase the signal budget available, while receiver sensitivity determines the minimum signal a device can decode.

Physical obstacles cause the most unpredictable losses. Drywall attenuates roughly 3 dB per wall, brick walls absorb about 6 dB, concrete walls block 10 dB or more, and metal surfaces can reflect or block signals almost entirely at 15 dB loss. The number of walls between router and device accumulates these losses quickly. Environmental factors like furniture, appliances, and other WiFi networks add interference that further reduces effective range.

WiFi Range by Frequency Band

Each WiFi frequency band offers different tradeoffs between range and speed. The table below summarizes typical indoor ranges for a standard router (20 dBm, 2 dBi antenna) in a typical home environment.

Band Typical Indoor Range Max Speed Interference Best For
2.4 GHz 35-50 m (115-165 ft) Up to 600 Mbps High (crowded band) Coverage, IoT devices
5 GHz 15-30 m (50-100 ft) Up to 3.5 Gbps Low Streaming, gaming
6 GHz 10-20 m (33-66 ft) Up to 9.6 Gbps Very low (new band) High-density, AR/VR

WiFi 6E and WiFi 7 routers operating on the 6 GHz band deliver the fastest speeds but cover the smallest area. For whole-home coverage, most users benefit from a dual-band or tri-band router that uses 2.4 GHz for distant devices and 5 GHz or 6 GHz for nearby high-bandwidth tasks.

Tips to Extend Your WiFi Coverage

If your calculated range does not cover your entire space, several strategies can help. Place your router in a central, elevated location away from walls and metal objects. Upgrading to a higher-gain external antenna can add 3-10 dBi to your signal budget. Mesh WiFi systems use multiple access points to blanket large homes with seamless coverage, typically adding 100-200 square meters per node.

WiFi extenders and repeaters can push coverage into dead zones, though they reduce throughput by half since they share bandwidth between receiving and retransmitting. Powerline adapters with WiFi provide an alternative by using your home electrical wiring to carry the signal to distant rooms. For multi-floor buildings, consider placing an access point on each floor connected by ethernet backhaul for the best performance.
