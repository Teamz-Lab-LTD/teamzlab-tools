How the Lawn to Garden Calculator Works

This calculator estimates the water, money and time you save by converting lawn to a productive garden or native landscape. Enter your lawn area in square feet, choose a conversion method and planting type, and the tool instantly calculates annual water savings based on typical lawn irrigation rates of 0.62 gallons per square foot per week compared to garden watering at roughly 0.30 gallons. Material requirements are computed based on your chosen method, whether that is sheet mulching with layers of cardboard and compost or building raised beds with imported soil. All calculations run entirely in your browser with no data sent anywhere.

Conversion Methods Compared

The four main methods each have trade-offs. Raised beds offer the most control over soil quality but cost the most upfront due to lumber and imported soil. Sheet mulching, also known as lasagna gardening, smothers the lawn with cardboard and layers of compost and mulch. It is the cheapest method and builds soil health over time but takes 3 to 6 months before planting. Sod removal strips the grass mechanically and is the fastest path to planting but requires renting equipment or heavy manual labor. The no-dig method uses a thick compost layer on top of the existing lawn, killing grass underneath while creating a ready-to-plant surface within weeks.

Water Savings from Lawn Conversion

Lawns are among the thirstiest landscapes. A typical lawn needs about 0.62 gallons per square foot per week during the growing season, adding up to thousands of gallons per year. Gardens, especially those with mulched beds and drip irrigation, use roughly half that amount. Native plant gardens use even less once established, needing supplemental water only during extended droughts. Converting just 500 square feet of lawn can save over 4,000 gallons of water annually, which translates to meaningful savings on your water bill and reduced strain on municipal water systems during peak summer demand.

Environmental Benefits of Removing Lawn

Beyond water savings, converting lawn eliminates the carbon footprint of mowing. Gas-powered lawn mowers produce approximately 80 pounds of CO2 per 1,000 square feet per year, plus additional pollutants including volatile organic compounds and particulate matter. Gardens also support biodiversity by providing habitat for pollinators, beneficial insects and birds. Native plant gardens in particular create mini ecosystems that support local wildlife. Vegetable gardens offset food transportation emissions by producing food steps from your kitchen rather than being shipped hundreds or thousands of miles from commercial farms.
